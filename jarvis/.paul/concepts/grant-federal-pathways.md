### Overview
NIH SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research) and STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer) are the primary federal mechanism for small businesses — including for-profit medical clinics — to obtain R&D funding. The med spa qualifies as a small business.

**Key Distinction:**
- **SBIR:** Small business does the work. PI must be primarily employed by the small business (>50% effort).
- **STTR:** Requires a formal research partnership with a university or nonprofit research institution. At least 40% of work must be done by the small business, at least 30% by the research partner. **This is the better mechanism for a med spa** because it allows partnering with Utah Tech University or SUU for credibility.

### CPT Code & Reimbursement Details

**CPT 99183 — Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (Physician Supervision)**
- Description: Physician or qualified healthcare professional attendance and supervision of hyperbaric oxygen therapy, per session
- Medicare 2025 rate (national): Approximately $30-50 per session (varies by locality)
- Utah Medicaid rate: Check current fee schedule at https://health.utah.gov/stplan/lookup/FeeScheduleDownload.php
- Historical Utah Medicaid limit: Up to 40 HBOT sessions per lifetime (per 2011 criteria document — verify current)
- Billing requirement: Continuous physician supervision required during session
- Covered indications (Medicaid): Non-healing wounds, decompression illness, gas gangrene, osteomyelitis, CO poisoning — NOT autism (off-label)
- **Bottom line:** CPT 99183 IS billable to Medicaid for approved indications. If the clinic also serves adult patients with diabetic wounds, osteomyelitis, etc. alongside the pediatric autism program, HBOT sessions for those patients ARE billable.

**CPT 97026 — Infrared Therapy (Photobiomodulation proxy)**
- Description: Infrared therapy, each 15 minutes
- Medicare 2025 status: CPT 97026 is classified as NON-COVERED under Medicare NCD for Infrared Therapy Devices (270.6)
- Utah Medicaid: Typically follows Medicare coverage policy for non-covered items
- **Practical implication:** Do not rely on 97026 for Medicaid billing of PBM. PBM is currently a cash-pay service for Medicaid patients.
- Note: Retinal photobiomodulation has its own new code (0936T, Category III) — not applicable here

**Hydrogen Therapy:**
- No CPT code exists for hydrogen therapy specifically
- Bill under physician E&M visit (99213-99215) for the consultation and medical oversight

## SECTION 11: FDA Breakthrough Device / De Novo Pathway

### MAJOR PRECEDENT FOUND: FDA Breakthrough Device for PBM + Autism

**JelikaLite's Cognilum System — FDA Breakthrough Device Designation (January 2022):**
- FDA granted Breakthrough Device Designation to JelikaLite's Cognilum System for the reduction of symptoms of moderate to severe autism spectrum disorder in pediatric patients ages 2-6
- Technology: Combines non-invasive brain stimulation with near-infrared light (transcranial photobiomodulation, tPBM) + EEG sensors + AI personalization
- Basis: Double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of 30 pediatric ASD patients showing safety and efficacy
- Clinical trial partner: SUNY Upstate Medical University (2023 trial announced)
- Status as of 2025: Anticipated FDA approval — check current status

**Significance for Jonathan's clinic:**
1. **FDA precedent exists** for transcranial PBM as a breakthrough treatment for pediatric autism
2. This precedent makes HBOT/PBM for autism a more fundable research area (reviewers see it as credible)
3. The Cognilum designation is for a specific proprietary device — but it validates the underlying science of PBM for autism
4. If Jonathan's PBM device is similar (transcranial infrared/near-infrared light delivery), a 510(k) clearance or De Novo pathway becomes more feasible

Sources:
- JelikaLite FDA designation: https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20220106005194/en/FDA-Grants-Breakthrough-Device-Designation-to-JelikaLite
- SUNY Upstate trial: https://www.upstate.edu/news/articles/2023/2023-12-15-wills.php

### FDA Pathway Options for the Med Spa

**Option 1: Use Existing Device Clearances (Easiest)**
- Many PBM/laser devices used in clinical practice already have 510(k) clearance for general use (pain, wound healing, physical therapy)
- Clinicians can use FDA-cleared devices off-label for autism (legal — this is standard medical practice)
- No additional FDA action required
- **Implication for billing:** Off-label use is legal but NOT reimbursable by insurance for the off-label indication — patients pay cash
- **Implication for grants:** Off-label use is publishable and grantable as clinical research

**Option 2: Seek 510(k) Clearance for Autism Indication**
- If the clinic develops a standardized PBM protocol (specific device + dose + duration) and wants to add autism to the device's cleared indications
- This is a device manufacturer pathway — requires working with the device manufacturer
- Timeline: 12-18 months; cost: $100K-$500K+
- **Only worth pursuing if the clinic is developing its own device or working closely with a manufacturer**

**Option 3: Apply for FDA Breakthrough Device Designation**
- Expedited review pathway for devices treating serious or life-threatening conditions
- Autism qualifies as a serious condition
- Requires: The device provides more effective treatment or diagnosis than available alternatives, or provides treatment when no alternative exists
- **For a PBM device specifically targeting the Cognilum protocol but as a clinic-developed system:** Potentially qualifying
- URL: https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/how-study-and-market-your-device/breakthrough-devices-program
- Timeline after designation: Still requires full PMA or De Novo review (1-3 additional years)

**Option 4: FDA Rare Pediatric Disease Grant (PRV) — Context**
- Rare Pediatric Disease Priority Review Vouchers (PRVs) go to drug/biologic/device developers for rare pediatric conditions
- Autism is NOT a rare disease (prevalence ~1:36) — does NOT qualify for Rare Pediatric Disease program
- NOT a relevant pathway for this clinic

### Strategic Assessment

**Realistic near-term FDA actions:**
1. Confirm that your PBM and HBOT equipment have appropriate FDA clearances for the indications you ARE billing (wound healing, physical therapy, etc.)
2. Operate the autism program under off-label use of cleared devices — this is legal and ethical with appropriate informed consent
3. Document outcomes rigorously — this data supports any future FDA regulatory strategy
4. Reference JelikaLite's Cognilum precedent in all grant applications as evidence that FDA recognizes PBM for autism as clinically plausible

**Long game (3-5 years if pursuing device development):**
- Partner with a PBM device manufacturer to develop autism-specific protocol
- Seek Breakthrough Device Designation together
- Use SBIR/STTR funding (when reauthorized) to fund the device development research

### 510(k) Status of Common Equipment
- Hyperbaric chambers: FDA Class II medical devices; must be FDA-cleared or exempt; check your specific manufacturer
- PBM/laser therapy devices: Most FDA Class II; cleared for wound healing, pain, inflammation
- Hydrogen therapy devices: Less regulatory clarity — verify with your specific device manufacturer
